Breaking Software, Building Quality: My Life in QA

May 8, 2025

I've taken the opportunity to reflect on my role as a Quality Assurance professional, how I contribute to a company and its technology team, what drew me to QA in the first place, and whether I truly enjoy what I do. The answer is a resounding yes. I love my job as a QA. My curiosity for how software works, paired with a drive to uncover issues others may not see, keeps me engaged. I enjoy thinking outside the box to find edge cases and break software in ways that might not have been anticipated by developers or users.

My attention to detail and analytical mindset has occasionally frustrated developers, especially when requirements weren't fully fleshed out, but in the end, it led to better, more resilient software. I was raised with the principle to always leave things better than I found them. That principle guides my work every day.

For the past six years, I’ve worked at a startup where I’ve gained a deep understanding of how software is built and maintained, and more importantly, how it serves users. I’ve learned to approach QA not just as a tester, but as an advocate for quality, usability, and the people behind the screens.

QA isn’t just about testing to meet the requirements, it’s about testing how the business will really use the software. A developer might not click the same button 15 times, hit the back button, then click it again, but a user might. And unless a QA Automation Engineer has done manual testing, they may not think to build tests for these scenarios up front.

Here’s how I approach various types of testing:

  • ○ Regression Testing: I build comprehensive test suites that examine every feature from multiple security levels. Just because a function works for one user role doesn’t mean it should be accessible, or even visible, to all roles. Ensuring that permissions and feature integrity are maintained is a top priority.
  • ○ Smoke Testing: After any deployment, I perform a high-level check of critical functions to ensure stability. If the core of the software is intact, then sanity testing can proceed.
  • ○ Sanity Testing: I verify that recent bug fixes haven’t introduced new issues and that existing functionality remains intact.
  • ○ Unit Testing: I look at features in isolation, ensuring the UI, functionality, APIs, links, and security hold up. For form-based features, I test for SQL injections to protect the system and its users.
  • ○ Integration Testing: This is where my business logic testing begins. If a feature isn’t intuitive or usable for me, how can we expect end users to succeed with it?

    • Quality Assurance is more than just a job title, it's a mindset. It’s about taking pride in the details, advocating for the user, and being a reliable voice between developers and stakeholders. Every test I perform, every bug I log, and every conversation I have with a developer is part of a larger goal: building software that is not only functional but exceptional. I’m proud to be a QA, and I look forward to continuing to grow in this field, helping teams deliver better software and stronger user experiences every step of the way.
